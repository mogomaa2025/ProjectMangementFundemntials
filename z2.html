Chapter 1.2: Project Management

Definition, Stakeholders, Knowledge Areas & Success Criteria

1.2 What is Project Management?
Project management is "the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements"

Project management is a comprehensive discipline that involves coordinating resources, managing timelines, and ensuring deliverables meet quality standards while satisfying stakeholder requirements[99][185]. It encompasses the strategic application of proven methodologies to achieve project objectives within defined constraints.

1.2A Project Stakeholders
Stakeholders are the people involved in or affected by project activities, and include various individuals and groups who have an interest in the project's outcome.

Understanding and managing stakeholders is crucial for project success[178][180]. Let's explore the different types of stakeholders through a house construction project example to understand their roles and expectations:

Project Sponsor

The individual or organization that initiates, funds, and champions the project, providing strategic direction and high-level decision-making authority.

Construction Example:

The potential new homeowners who would be paying for the house. They define requirements, make key decisions, and provide project funding.

Project Team

The core group of professionals directly responsible for executing project work and delivering the final product or service.

Construction Example:

Several construction workers, electricians, and carpenters. They would need to know if the required materials and equipment will be at the construction site.

Support Staff

Individuals who provide indirect support through administrative, coordination, or specialized services that enable project success.

Construction Example:

The contractor's administrative assistant that would support the project by coordinating meetings between the buyers, the contractor, suppliers, and other parties.

Suppliers

External organizations or vendors that provide materials, equipment, or services necessary for project completion.

Construction Example:

Suppliers would expect exact details on the items (wood, windows, flooring, appliances, and other materials) they need to provide, and where and when to deliver those items.

Opponents

Individuals or groups who may resist or oppose the project due to potential negative impacts on their interests or well-being.

Construction Example:

A neighbor might oppose the project because the workers make so much noise that she cannot concentrate on her work at home.

Stakeholder Management Success

Effective stakeholder management involves identifying, analyzing, and engaging all stakeholders throughout the project lifecycle[182]. This proactive approach helps minimize conflicts, ensures requirements are met, and builds support for project success.

1.2B Project Management Knowledge Areas
Project management knowledge areas describe the key competencies that project managers must develop to successfully manage projects across all phases and processes.

The Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK) defines 10 core knowledge areas that represent fundamental skills and expertise needed for effective project management. Each area focuses on specific aspects and provides processes and best practices.

1. Project Scope Management

Project scope management involves defining and managing all the work required to complete the project successfully. This includes creating detailed scope statements and controlling scope changes.

2. Project Time Management

Project time management includes estimating how long it will take to complete the work, developing an acceptable project schedule, and ensuring timely completion of the project.

3. Project Cost Management

Project cost management consists of preparing and managing the budget for the project. This involves cost estimation, budgeting, and cost control processes.

4. Project Quality Management

Project quality management ensures that the project will satisfy the stated or implied needs for which it was undertaken. This includes quality planning, assurance, and control.

5. Project Human Resource Management

Project human resource management is concerned with making effective use of the people involved with the project. This includes team acquisition, development, and management.

6. Project Communications Management

Project communications management involves generating, collecting, disseminating, and storing project information. Effective communication ensures stakeholder alignment throughout the project.

7. Project Risk Management

Project risk management includes identifying, analyzing, and responding to risks related to the project. This proactive approach minimizes negative impacts and maximizes opportunities.

8. Project Procurement Management

Project procurement management involves acquiring or procuring goods and services for a project from outside the performing organization. This includes vendor selection and contract management.

9. Project Stakeholder Management

Project stakeholder management includes identifying and analyzing stakeholder needs while managing and controlling their engagement throughout the life of the project.

10. Project Integration Management

Project integration management is an overarching function that affects and is affected by all of the other knowledge areas. It coordinates and unifies all project elements.

The Central Role of Integration Management

Project Integration Management serves as the coordinating function that brings together all other knowledge areas. Unlike the other nine areas that focus on specific aspects, integration management ensures that all project components work together harmoniously to achieve project objectives. It's the "glue" that holds everything together.

1.2C Project Success

How do you define the success or failure of a project?

Case Study: Desktop Computer Upgrade Project

Let's illustrate project success criteria with an example: Upgrading 500 desktop computers within three months for $300,000. This real-world scenario helps us understand the multiple dimensions of project success.

1. Meeting Project Goals

The project met scope, time, and cost goals. If all 500 computers were upgraded and met other scope requirements, the work was completed in three months or less, and the cost was $300,000 or less, you could consider the project successful.

2. Customer/Sponsor Satisfaction

The project satisfied the customer/sponsor. If the customers were not happy with important aspects of the project, it would be deemed a failure. Conversely, a project might not meet initial goals, but the customer could still be very satisfied if the team was polite and helped solve work-related problems.

3. Meeting Main Objectives

The results met the main objective. Success could mean making or saving a certain amount of money, providing a good return on investment, or simply making the sponsors happy with the overall outcome and business value delivered.

Key Success Insight

Project success is multi-dimensional and goes beyond simply meeting the triple constraint of scope, time, and cost. True success requires balancing technical achievement with stakeholder satisfaction and business value delivery. A project that delivers perfect technical results but leaves stakeholders dissatisfied cannot be considered truly successful.

Success Factors in Practice:

Technical Success

Meeting scope, time, and cost objectives

Stakeholder Success

Customer and sponsor satisfaction

Business Success

Achieving strategic objectives and ROI

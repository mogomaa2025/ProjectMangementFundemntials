Project Management Fundamentals

Understanding Projects, Attributes, and the Triple Constraint

1.1 What is a Project?
A project is "a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result."

Operations, on the other hand, is work done in organizations to sustain the business. Projects are different from operations in that they end when their objectives have been reached or the project has been terminated[161][162].

Key Distinctions: Projects vs. Operations

Projects

Temporary endeavors with defined start and end dates, creating unique deliverables

Operations

Ongoing work that sustains the business through repetitive, continuous activities

Project Completion

Projects end when objectives are achieved or when terminated

1.1A Project Attributes

Projects come in all shapes and sizes. The following attributes help define a project further:

Unique Purpose

A project has a unique purpose. Every project should have a well-defined objective. Projects result in a unique product, service, or result.

Example:

To create a collaborative report with ideas from people throughout the company.

Temporary Nature

A project is temporary. A project has a definite beginning and end.

Example:

In the IT collaboration project, Anne might form a team to work immediately and expect a report and executive presentation in one month.

Progressive Elaboration

A project is developed using progressive elaboration. Details become clearer as the project progresses through phases.

Example:

If initial ideas don't clearly address business strategy, the team might prepare a questionnaire to improve input quality.

Resource Requirements

A project requires resources, often from various areas. Resources include people, hardware, software, and other assets.

Example:

For the IT collaboration project, people from IT, marketing, sales, distribution, and other areas would need to work together.

Primary Customer/Sponsor

A project should have a primary customer or sponsor. The project sponsor usually provides direction and funding. Executive support is crucial to project success.

Example:

The sponsor of a project to improve online product sales would be the vice president of sales.

Involves Uncertainty

A project involves uncertainty. Because every project is unique, it can be difficult to define objectives clearly, estimate duration, or determine costs.

Example:

External factors cause uncertainty, such as a project team member needing unplanned time off.

1.1B Project Constraints (The Triple Constraint)

To create a successful project, a project manager must consider the triple constraint - the three fundamental limitations that define project boundaries[161][163]:

Success Definition

Successful project management means meeting all three goals (scope, time, and cost)—and satisfying the project's sponsor!

The Three Core Constraints:

Scope

What work will be done as part of the project? What unique product, service, or result does the customer or sponsor expect from the project?

Key Questions:

What deliverables are included? What are the project boundaries? Who defines scope changes?

Time

How long should it take to complete the project? What is the project's schedule? How will the team track actual schedule performance?

Key Questions:

What is the deadline? What are key milestones? Who can approve schedule changes?

Cost

What should it cost to complete the project? What is the project's budget? Who can authorize changes to the budget?

Key Questions:

What is the total budget? How will costs be tracked? Who has budget authority?

Managing Trade-offs

Managing the triple constraint involves making trade-offs between scope, time, and cost goals for a project[161][162]. Changes to one constraint will inevitably impact the other two, requiring careful balance and stakeholder communication.

Additional Constraint Factors

Other elements can also play significant roles beyond the traditional triple constraint:

Quality

Quality is often a key factor in projects, as is customer or sponsor satisfaction. Quality standards must be maintained while balancing the triple constraint.

Resources

Resources are the main concern. For example, the entertainment industry often needs particular actors for movies or television shows.

Risk

Risk can also affect major project decisions. A company might wait to start a project until the risks are at an acceptable level.

Key Insight

While the triple constraint provides a fundamental framework, successful project management requires considering these additional factors to ensure comprehensive project success. Modern project management recognizes that quality, resources, and risk are equally important constraints that must be managed alongside scope, time, and cost.
